# Wedding Speech Playbook

## Opening — one-sentence relationship
The first sentence is your job: tell the room who you are to the couple, in one specific sentence. Not "I've known Tom for many years" — "I met Tom on day one of university when he stole my laundry basket." Specificity buys you the room's attention faster than emotion does.

## Pacing — slower than you think
Wedding nerves push speakers to 180–200 words per minute. The target is 130–150. The room is drunk-ish, the acoustics are usually bad, and they need to follow the joke setup before they can laugh at the payoff. If your transcript clocks above 160 WPM the single highest-value fix is to deliberately pause after every punchline.

## Structure — three stories, one theme
Best speeches pick one theme about the person ("Tom is the most stubborn loyal man I know") and tell exactly three short stories that prove it. Avoid the trap of listing twelve memories — the audience will not retain twelve. They will retain one trait and three stories. Five minutes total. Time it.

## The bride/groom rule
If you are toasting one partner, mention the other within the first 90 seconds and the last 30 seconds. Doing it only at the end feels tacked on. Doing it only at the start makes the toast feel one-sided.

## Avoiding the inside joke trap
If only the wedding party laughs at a joke, you've failed the room. Cut anything that requires being in the group chat. Specificity is good; in-jokes are not specificity, they are exclusion.

## Land the toast
End on a specific raised-glass instruction, not a sentimental fade. "To Tom and Sara — may the dog continue to prefer Sara." Hand the room a clear action. Pause before "to" so they know to lift their glass.
