# Conference Talk Playbook

## The single-takeaway rule
The audience will remember one thing from your talk. Decide what that thing is before you write a single slide. Every section should reinforce it. If a slide doesn't ladder back to the takeaway, cut it.

## Opening — hook, not history
Don't start with "today I'll be talking about…" Start with a story, a counterintuitive claim, or a live demo. The first 60 seconds decide whether the audience pulls out their phones. Earn their attention; don't expect it.

## Pacing — slower than rehearsal
Live audiences need 10–15% more processing time than your living room does. If you nailed it at 140 WPM in rehearsal, target 125 in the room. Pauses after each main point let the audience write down what mattered.

## Slide density
One idea per slide. If you can't read your slide from the back of the room, the back of the room can't read your slide. Numbers and short phrases beat sentences. The slide supports your voice; it does not replace it.

## Handling the live demo
If you're demoing something live, rehearse the failure case. Have a backup screenshot. If it breaks, narrate what should have happened — the audience forgives the bug, not the silence.

## Q&A — repeat the question
Always repeat the question back before answering. Two reasons: the room behind the asker can't hear them, and it gives you five seconds to think. If you don't know the answer, say "I don't know — let's find out after" and move on.
