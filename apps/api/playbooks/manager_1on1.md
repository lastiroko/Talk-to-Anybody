# Manager 1:1 Playbook

## The first 60 seconds — status, then ask
Open with a 30-second status summary, then ask one specific question. Do not bury the ask under five minutes of context. Managers process status updates at 2x speed in their head — they want to know where you're stuck.

## Bring the decision, not the dilemma
"I'm thinking we should do X because Y, but I'm uncertain about Z — does that match your read?" beats "What do you think we should do?" by a country mile. You came prepared, you have a thesis, you're seeking confirmation or correction. That's leverage.

## Cadence — slow and certain
1:1s reward calm. If you sound rushed your manager will assume the situation is worse than it is. Aim for 110–130 WPM, with deliberate pauses after the decisive sentence. Pace signals confidence in a way that word choice does not.

## The career-stuff parking lot
Save career, growth, and feedback topics for the last ten minutes. If you lead with them, you starve the operational topics that have time-sensitive blocking implications. Put one growth topic at the end of every other meeting.

## Owning a missed deliverable
Lead with "I missed X. Here's what I learned and what I'm doing differently." Don't open with the excuse. Don't bury it three sentences in. The framing is: I own it, I diagnosed it, I'm fixing it — in that order, in three sentences max.

## The escalation ask
If you need air cover from your manager, say so explicitly: "I need you to talk to [team] about [thing]" — not "I wonder if maybe there's something we could…" Indirect asks waste the meeting; explicit asks are easier for the manager to act on.
